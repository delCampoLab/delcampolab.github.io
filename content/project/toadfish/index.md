Calcium carbonate production in the ocean is dominated by planktonic microeukaryotes such as coccolithophores and foraminifera. However, an alternative and remarkable carbon sink — piscine carbonate — has largely been overlooked. Marine teleost fish precipitate CaCO₃ in their intestines as part of their osmoregulatory strategy. Based on estimates of marine fish biomass (~800–2,000 million tons), this carbonate precipitation can represent up to 15% of total calcium carbonate deposition in the ocean.

Ocean acidification due to elevated atmospheric CO₂ is predicted to decrease CaCO₃ production rates in calcifying marine plankton and corals. Fish, however, use endogenous CO₂ to produce HCO₃⁻ ions at very high concentrations within the gut lumen, so their contribution to carbonate production is expected to increase under future ocean conditions. Despite carbonate precipitation being critical for fish rehydration and survival, the molecular mechanisms that regulate this reaction remain unknown. Strikingly, no candidate genes involved in this process have been identified from the genome or transcriptome of the Gulf Toadfish (*Opsanus beta*), the model organism for this process.

This leads us to hypothesise that it is the gut microbiota — rather than the fish itself — that is responsible for the carbonate precipitation. The involvement of bacteria in calcium carbonate deposition is well-established in marine environments, and bacteria have been linked to the development of kidney stones in mammals. We are testing this hypothesis using a combination of genomics, transcriptomics, and microbiology to identify the microbial candidates driving piscine carbonate production.
